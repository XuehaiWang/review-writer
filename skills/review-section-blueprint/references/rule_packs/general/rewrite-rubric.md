# Rewrite Rubric

## Where To Find Information In The Paper

Use the paper structure to locate evidence before drafting. Do not rely only on the abstract when a full text, figures, tables, or supporting information are available.

- Title: identify the topic, object of study, or headline claim; verify all details elsewhere before using them.
- Abstract: extract the main advance, system, headline metric, and author-framed significance; treat it as a map, not the only evidence source.
- Introduction: use for the knowledge gap, prior limitation, and why the work matters; avoid importing broad background unless it explains the paper's placement in the review.
- Results/Findings section: use as the primary source for method, object of study, design or approach, evidence, controls, scope, performance, comparisons, and interpretation.
- Tables and figures: use for exact values, trends, comparisons, and structure/behavior relationships.
- Supporting or supplementary material: use for full conditions, extended scope, weaker or failed cases, control details, and practical caveats.
- Conclusion: use for the authors' own framing only after verifying the claim against the results/findings.

## Extraction Checklist

Capture these facts before drafting. Keep only source-supported details.

1. Citation identity: authors, year, and a short identifier for the object of study or claim.
2. Review placement: why this paper matters to the section's argument.
3. Core object: the method, system, artifact, or subject of study.
4. Design rationale: why the approach was structured this way, if stated.
5. Method: decisive procedure, technique, or experiment.
6. Key result: quantitative or qualitative performance and comparison baseline where available.
7. Scope and boundary: what was covered, what wasn't, operating conditions, or limits.
8. Interpretation: the paper's explanation for its result, and how strongly it is supported.
9. Limitation or caveat: unresolved question, narrow scope, weak evidence, or missing controls.
10. Review relevance: how the finding supports, contrasts with, extends, or limits the review's narrative.

## Evidence Strength

Assign each important claim one evidence mode before writing:

- `reported_data`: directly reported results, controls, or measurements from the source.
- `author_inference`: the source authors' own proposal, analogy, or interpretation that is consistent with the data but not directly demonstrated.
- `unsupported`: missing from the source material available to you.

Use evidence-bearing verbs for `reported_data`, cautious verbs for `author_inference`, and omit or flag `unsupported` claims.

## Compression Rules

- Lead with the paper's distinct contribution, not the field's generic background.
- Combine method and result when possible: "Using [method], the authors showed..."
- Retain numbers that change the interpretation; drop numbers that only decorate the claim.
- Replace long inventories with representative examples unless the full scope is the key result.
- Keep interpretations conditional when the source is conditional: use "suggested", "consistent with", or "attributed to" instead of "proved" when evidence is indirect.
- Preserve negative or limiting findings if they affect how the paper should be interpreted in a review.
- Avoid repeating the paper title in paraphrased form.
- Keep the main limitation in the same sentence or immediately adjacent sentence when it changes how the advance should be judged.
- Do not generalize one case, sample, or condition to another without qualification.

## Paragraph Template

Use this logic, but do not force the wording:

```text
[Citation/authors if needed] reported/developed [main system or contribution], which [review-relevant advance]. Using [key method or evidence], they showed [central result with values when available]. The study [interpretation or scope boundary], but [main limitation/caveat if relevant], making it [specific role in the review narrative].
```

## Quality Gate

Before returning the paragraph, answer internally:

- Does the paragraph say what was studied, how it was studied, what was found, and why it matters?
- Are all central results and identifiers preserved?
- Are unsupported claims removed?
- Is the causal language no stronger than the evidence?
- Would a reader understand the paper's role in the review without reading the original abstract?
- Is the paragraph concise enough to fit into a review section without sounding like a standalone abstract?

If any answer is no, revise before responding.
