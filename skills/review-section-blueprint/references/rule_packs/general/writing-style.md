# General Review Style

Use these rules for any academic literature review, regardless of subject area. Replace bracketed placeholders with the terminology, entities, and metrics that are actually native to the review's field.

## Voice And Diction

- Write formal academic review prose, not an abstract, sales pitch, or lab notebook entry.
- Lead with the paper's central conclusion or contribution, not its title paraphrased.
- Prefer precise verbs: `reported`, `observed`, `showed`, `demonstrated`, `supported`, `proposed`, `interpreted`, `found`, `established`, or `identified`.
- Use `demonstrated` only when the paper provides direct evidence or a clear result. Use `is consistent with`, `is proposed to`, `may involve`, or `was interpreted as` for indirect or author-inferred claims.
- Avoid ornamental hedging such as "it is interesting to note" and generic openings such as "recent years have seen".
- Do not write from the source authors' first-person perspective. Replace "we developed" with "the authors developed" or a citation-neutral construction.

## Information To Preserve

For a single-paper paragraph, preserve these details when source-supported and review-relevant:

- The core method/approach, the main input/object of study, and the main output/result, at whatever level of specificity the field uses to distinguish one contribution from another.
- The enabling technique, tool, model, system, or apparatus, and any secondary component that changes the outcome.
- Scale, setting, or conditions when the source frames the work as notably practical, efficient, or scalable relative to the field's norms.
- Quantitative or qualitative performance metrics central to the claim (whatever the field's standard metrics are — accuracy, yield, effect size, throughput, etc.).
- Meaningful contrasts between subclasses of the object of study (e.g. one input type vs. another) when the paper uses them to define scope.
- The main limitation: narrow scope, unfavorable conditions, unresolved question, weak evidence, or unaddressed edge case.

## Precision

- Distinguish the field's standard categories relevant to this paper (method/approach type, object-of-study class, enabling technique, output class) as far as the source supports.
- Name specific mechanistic/procedural steps only when the source directly supports them.
- Do not promote a plausible explanation into established fact. Interpretive prose must inherit the evidence strength assigned during extraction (see the extraction rubric).
- Pair each causal or mechanistic claim with an evidence-status modifier unless direct evidence supports stronger wording.
- Do not move details, conditions, or values from one paper to another.
- If the paper lacks direct evidence for its explanation, state that the explanation was proposed or inferred rather than established.

## Scope Compression

- Do not reproduce a data table or exhaustive list in prose.
- Compress scope into class-level trends, boundaries, and one representative anchor if needed.
- A useful scope sentence names the relevant input class, comparison class, output class, and a limitation when those are available.
- Do not call a method broad, general, efficient, or highly effective without numbers or demonstrated class-level support.
- Keep negative examples or weak cases when they define the method's boundary.

## Comparison And Practicality

- When comparing methods, state the shared benchmark first, then identify the differentiating variable.
- Use like-for-like comparison axes appropriate to the field: input class, output class, approach/mechanism, evidence strength, robustness, operational simplicity, or downstream value.
- Use practicality adjectives only when the source provides operational evidence (scale, simplicity, robustness, resource cost, reproducibility).
- If a method requires unusual resources, careful handling, or lacks scale/generality data, keep that caveat near the claimed advantage.
- Phrase advantages as class-specific when the data support only a class-specific benefit, rather than implying general superiority.

## Prohibited Patterns

Avoid these unless the paper directly supports the exact claim:

- "has attracted increasing attention"
- "recent years have seen"
- "interesting results" / "interesting findings"
- "efficient", "excellent", "high-performing", or "broad scope" without numbers or demonstrated classes
- "proceeds smoothly" (or field-equivalent vague success language)
- "novel" without naming the novelty
- "to the best of our knowledge"
- "review articles demonstrate" for primary experimental/observational outcomes

## One-Paragraph Review Pattern

Prefer this information order:

1. Contribution identity and source's role.
2. Method/approach, object-of-study, and output classes.
3. Quantitative or qualitative outcome and scope boundary.
4. Supporting evidence or author-proposed rationale, marked by evidence strength.
5. Limitation, or the reason the paper matters to this review section.

The paragraph should read as synthesis of evidence, not as a paper abstract or a list of details.

Use a compact rhythm:

```text
[Method/approach identity and object of study]. [Key evidence plus scope or outcome compression]. [Evidence-strength or comparison qualifier]. [Limitation-linked takeaway].
```
